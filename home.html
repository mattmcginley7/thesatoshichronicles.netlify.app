What Even is Money?

Before money, people would trade their goods or services with other's goods or services. Imagine you as a farmer trying to pay for your haircut in chickens. Then imagine trying to sell a whole chicken for a couple of apples. This was a mess. So creating a system of value that was always relative is very important.

Throughout history, humans have used many different things as money. Those who lived in mountains would trade rocks with those who lived near the beach that had seashells. Even feathers were used as money. Yes, it was that dumb and sucked, but it's all we had.

Eventually, we discovered gold. We ended up realizing that gold is something that is hard to find that everyone wants. This was a big step in the evolution of money. It's just a dumb metal, but in terms of the attributes we care about when it comes to a system of money, like being scarce, divisible, and people wanted it for itself, gold was the best thing we had.

Gold is not perfect though. Gold is hidden all throughout the earth, and we're not exactly sure how much of it exists. So the amount of gold, (or its scarcity if you're looking at the chart) is always changing, but in pretty small amounts that keeps the value of gold pretty stable.

Imagine if the amount of gold on all the earth doubled every year and that's what we used to base our money on. Each year, the amount of money that you had would be worth half! Let's say year 1 you had $100,000. Year 2 it's only actually worth $50k! It would be impossible to save.

But the actual amount of gold that we find each year compared to all that gold that exists is around 2% or so, so over the short and medium term, gold ended up being pretty stable to base money on! In that scenario where you had $100,000 in year 1, you'd have $98,000 in actual value year 2. This is the importance of scarcity, it's one of the most important attributes of money.

But, gold is not without its limitations. It is not easy to move in large quantities. It sucks to break down into small pieces. So paper money is born to try to solve these problems.

The United States decides to tie the amount of dollars that exist to the amount of gold that exists. Until 1971.

In 1971, the United States was fighting in the Vietnam War. Imagine you are the president of a really big country, the most powerful in the world, currently at war. You are given a printer that can make UNLIMITED money. Instead of running around door to door collecting money from your own citizens, you can just create a shit ton of money instantly that can pay for tanks, bombs, weapons, and soldiers. To fund the war, are you going door to door asking for tax money or using the infinity printer?

Yeah, you guessed it, we used that machine (and still lost). What is that machine exactly? It's the dollar not tied to anything, therefore those in control of the financial system can create infinite amounts of money for free. And in fact, we haven't stopped using it 50+ years later.

What exactly is the consequence of this? The value of money across time from year to year continues to lose its value at faster rates. If everyone knows the money sucks and will keep being worth less, people avoid saving in it. Then things that aren't meant to really be money, such as homes, end up getting inflated in value. It's harder to create homes than it is money, so it keeps its value. But homes should be meant to be lived in, not meant to be ways to maintain your wealth.

In 1971, the year we created this infinite printing machine, you could buy the average home in about 2.5 years of the average income at the time. So if you were making $8000 per year, a home cost about $20,000. Today? The average salary is about $40,000 and the average new home is nearly $400,000, that is 10 years, or 4 times more difficult than 1971.

Again, gold is just a dumb metal. It's not special. It is however better than money being decided arbitrarily with an infinite money printing machine.

So wtf does this have to do with Bitcoin? EVERYTHING.

Unforgeable Costliness

Bitcoin uses energy to produce Bitcoin. If a Bitcoin could be created for free, anyone would be capable of co-opting the network. The lack of an energy requirement could make it easier for attackers to perform malicious activities on the network, such as double spending or denial of service attacks. These types of attacks can potentially harm the integrity and security of the network, and the energy requirement helps to prevent them by making them too costly to be worth the effort. Overall, the energy requirement for bitcoin mining serves as an important security and stability mechanism for the bitcoin network. Without it, the network would likely be much less secure and less able to maintain the value of the currency.

This is a feature for gold too, but only in terms of it maintaining its value, not for actual applications of gold. Think about it. There are use cases to gold, it's important in building certain technology like computers. Having more gold and cheaper gold would be a great thing for actually using it for these purposes.

This is also why it's a great thing that Bitcoin doesn't have physical "intrinsic" value. Bitcoin exists as money only, so it doesn't have to compete with other physical use cases that are better off if the price is cheaper. The price of Bitcoin could reach 1 million dollars and still be just as useful as it is today. Gold would be hoarded and unavailable for use in tech.

Open source invites the best people in the world to come in and make change if they wish. And simultaneously, it allows its own users to decide what they like and what they don't want.

Closed source doesn't get the best minds. Only the ones in charge. And you're given what you're given as a user. You don't have any say.

Now think about the long term implications of closed source technologies versus open sourced technologies. One is constantly reiterating on itself, with the best minds motivated to create to give people exactly what they want. These technologies are essentially unstoppable.The same level of inspiration, drive and excitement to build and create new things for closed source naturally pales in comparison to that of open source by design.

Money is getting faster, cheaper to send, and more open every day in Bitcoin. Dollars remain the exact same.

Decentralized

All of these properties of Bitcoin couldn't exist without its ability to be decentralized.

Centralized means everything is under control by a singlar entity. They get to make all the changes. All the edits. It's god mode. They have all the power. And they tend to have a singlar point of weakness/vulnerability. Imagine robbing a bank vault. If you're trying to attack it, you just go for the vault. That is an example of money that is centralized to a single location.

Decentralized is as if there is no bank vault. You need to go door to door to rob everyone. But in the case of Bitcoin, not only do you have to go door to door, you have to simultaneously go door to door of 51% of everyone that goes to that bank.

With millions of Bitcoin miners and nodes all of over the world, many of them anonymous, there is not a single possible way to ever attack the actual Bitcoin network. It is truly an impossible task. No way to take over the network and make changes or edits.

In the case of the vault, this is exactly the problem with gold. Yes, gold can exist outside of vaults, but that's almost always where it ends up, especially for goverments with their reserves. And then we fall back into the problem where gold can be confiscated if they're left in these vaults.
