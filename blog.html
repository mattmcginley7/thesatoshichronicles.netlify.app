The Store of Value Wars: One Asset to Rule Them All

How Bitcoin stacks up against every other place to park your wealth

Money really only has two jobs: move value through space and keep value through time. We all grasp the first job because sending money is part of daily life. The second job—preserving purchasing power—is where the hidden damage happens. You can still hold the same stack of dollars, yet every item you want costs more because the supply of dollars keeps expanding.

When prices rise, it is tempting to blame the coffee shop, the landlord, or the hospital. Competition keeps most businesses from gouging, though; the real culprit is that the money supply grows, so each dollar buys less. Imagine a town with $10 per person and apples priced at $1. If the money supply doubles, each person now has $20, and apples become $2 to keep the same share of purchasing power. The seller is not suddenly greedy—the money just became worth less.

Here is the kicker: if no one could create more money, prices would naturally fall over time as technology makes producing and delivering goods cheaper. Better tools, richer soil, autonomous transport—each innovation lowers costs. In a fixed-supply money world, those efficiency gains flow to everyone as lower prices.

The wealthy already know this game. They avoid holding cash by buying assets: gold, commodities, bonds, stocks, real estate, and increasingly Bitcoin. Every asset comes with trade-offs: volatility, exposure to management decisions, liquidity limits, or reliance on market hours. The question is whether one asset best fulfills the goal of maintaining and growing value through time.

Designing the perfect store of value

To protect value across time, an asset needs a few core qualities:

  • Fixed supply: New units cannot be created at will. Bitcoin caps out at 21 million, enforced by its decentralized network. Gold supply grows every year, and new shares or new dollars can be issued whenever issuers decide.
  • User control: Holders must be able to custody and secure the asset themselves. Bitcoin allows self-custody with a phone or hardware wallet; stocks sit on exchanges that can gate access, and large amounts of gold are cumbersome to secure.
  • Always-on liquidity: You should be able to use or sell it anytime. Real estate can take months to sell, and stocks trade only during market hours. Bitcoin trades 24/7/365 globally.

Across the world, assets compete to be the preferred vault for wealth: real estate and bonds sit in the hundreds of trillions, equities hover around a hundred trillion, and gold commands tens of trillions. Bitcoin, the contender purpose-built as digital property with a hard cap, is still under two trillion.

Markets are Darwinian. Over time, the assets with the strongest store-of-value traits rise to the top. If Bitcoin truly is the superior option, the flow of wealth toward it is only beginning. Those who recognize and act on that reality early are best positioned to benefit.

The OFFICIAL “Is this is a dumb question” Q&A for Bitcoin

This is your safe place to ask the “obvious” Bitcoin questions. Every answer below is written to be direct, plain English, and free of jargon so you can build confidence in your understanding step by step.

Is Bitcoin a scam?

No, Bitcoin is not a scam. For decades, computer programmers have been trying to create a money that uses cryptography to secure it. The idea is if a currency can be created that no one can steal or control, a better system of money can exist. Bitcoin was the first successful one because Satoshi, the creator of Bitcoin, solved a famous computer science challenge called the Byzantine Generals Problem—how to get computers that do not trust each other to agree on the same truth.

Can I only buy a whole Bitcoin?

Fortunately, you can buy any amount of Bitcoin you want. You can own tiny fractions. If Bitcoin is $100,000, you could buy $100 worth, $10 worth, or even just a few cents worth and still participate.

Where can I buy Bitcoin?

You can buy Bitcoin on exchanges, where you trade your local currency for Bitcoin. Some of the most popular ones are Coinbase, Gemini, and Binance.

Why should I buy Bitcoin?

The shortest and simplest answer: Bitcoin is likely to become more valuable over time, while the currency you use today (dollars, euros, pesos, etc.) will likely buy less over time.

Why is this?

Money has certain properties—it should be scarce, secure, and easy to send and receive. Bitcoin is stronger than traditional currencies in each of these areas, so more people are choosing it for long-term savings.

Isn’t Bitcoin volatile?

Yes, Bitcoin is volatile. The price can swing significantly in a short time. Because of this, invest with a plan. Ask yourself, “If the price drops a lot tomorrow, will I still be okay?” If the answer is no, invest a smaller amount that lets you stay calm and patient for the long term.

What if I want to sell?

The same exchange that lets you buy Bitcoin will let you sell it back into your local currency when you decide to.

When should I sell?

Ask yourself: when will the money you are switching back into be better than Bitcoin? If you believe Bitcoin still offers stronger money properties, selling may not make sense yet.

When should I buy?

If the money you are currently using is not serving you as well as Bitcoin can, consider buying Bitcoin as soon as possible so that more of your savings live in the better form of money.

I understand Bitcoin is better, but I still want to take some profit.

It is your money and your Bitcoin, so always do what is best for you. Just know that selling for no clear reason is often met with regret later. Bitcoin has produced the greatest returns of any asset in history, and those who benefited most decided Bitcoin was their primary long-term money.

Keep asking questions, keep learning, and move at the pace that lets you sleep well at night.
